# Website Design: CampuX

## 1. Website Overview

**Website Name:** CampuX  
**Website Type:** Campus second-hand marketplace and wanted board  
**Target Users:** University students who want to buy, sell, reserve, or request second-hand items within a trusted campus-only environment

CampuX is designed as a student-first marketplace, not a generic e-commerce site. The platform supports both selling and buying workflows, plus a wanted/request flow for users who are looking for specific items. The current interface combines browsing, publishing, messaging, checkout, and order tracking into one product.

The visual direction is intentionally editorial and showcase-like, with soft transparency, bold hero typography, and card-based content blocks. The layout is built to feel modern and premium while still being practical for daily student use.

## 3. Wireframe Descriptions

### 3.1 Home Page Wireframe

**Top Header**
- The CampuX logo sits on the left with a short brand statement beside it.
- Search and utility controls are grouped on the right.
- A compact `My` entry opens Messages, Cart, and Orders.

**Hero Section**
- The left side contains a large headline, a short supporting sentence, and action buttons.
- The right side contains a visual campus card that reinforces trust and location context.
- The hero uses generous spacing and a premium editorial layout.

**Mode Switcher**
- Three compact chips sit below the hero: Browse, Sell, and Wanted.
- Only one mode is active at a time, making the page feel more like a guided experience.

**Marketplace Block**
- Filters sit in a single horizontal tool row.
- Listing cards appear in a two-column grid on desktop.
- Cart and recent messages live in a sidebar to the right.

**Seller and Wanted Entry**
- The seller form and wanted board are displayed as separate focused sections.
- Each section is revealed as a destination, not as clutter on the first screen.

### 3.2 Marketplace Wireframe

**Filter Row**
- Search, category, condition, sort, and price all appear together.
- The row is lightweight and does not dominate the page.

**Listing Cards**
- Each card includes a large image, badge, title, condition, tags, price, and location.
- Hover states provide a subtle lifted effect.
- Favorite and cart actions stay visible without making the card look crowded.

**Sidebar**
- The cart summary appears as a compact panel.
- A message preview panel sits below it, creating a useful side column rather than extra noise.

### 3.3 Seller Studio Wireframe

**Form Layout**
- The posting form uses a clear two-column structure for key fields.
- A dedicated upload area supports photo selection and mobile camera capture.
- AI helper copy is placed near the action buttons rather than competing with the form.

**Interaction**
- Users can add a photo, generate a draft, then publish directly into the marketplace.
- After publishing, the page returns to the browsing area.

### 3.4 Wanted Board Wireframe

**Wanted Board**
- Wanted requests appear as a grid of request cards.
- Budget is visually highlighted because it is the most important decision cue.

**Wanted Form**
- The form is placed beside the board to encourage immediate posting.
- It mirrors the seller form, which helps users understand both directions of trading.

### 3.5 Checkout Wireframe

**Main Column**
- Order summary lists the chosen items.
- A form collects contact and handoff details.
- The confirmation state replaces the form after submission.

**Right Column**
- A compact order summary reinforces the total and selected items.
- The layout feels like a real step in the transaction flow rather than a demo dialog.

### 3.6 User Dashboard Wireframe

**Top Summary**
- Welcome message
- Verification state
- Counts for active listings, orders, saved items, and unread messages

**Activity Blocks**
- My Listings
- My Orders
- Saved Items
- Recent Messages
- Notifications

### 3.7 FAQ Wireframe

**Structure**
- Page title and short explainer at the top
- FAQ questions grouped by theme
- Accordion-style answers below
- A help contact box at the bottom

## 4. Full Sample Content

### 4.1 Homepage Sample Content

The homepage presents CampuX as a campus-only marketplace for students. The navigation keeps search and quick access tools visible, while the hero section uses a short headline, a supportive sentence, and two primary actions to move users into browsing or joining the market. A short guidance line tells users they can search, save, buy, or post in a few taps. The page does not overload the user with item-by-item copy; instead, it introduces the kind of marketplace they can expect, with common student categories such as electronics, dorm essentials, fashion, and textbooks represented in the visual layout.

### 4.2 User Dashboard Sample Content

The dashboard is written as a compact account overview. It summarizes active listings, unread messages, saved items, and orders so the user can quickly see what needs attention. A welcome panel introduces the account state, while the activity area highlights the most important actions and reminders. The wording is kept practical and student-oriented so the page feels like a real control center rather than a decorative summary.

### 4.3 FAQ Sample Content

The FAQ section answers the most common questions about access, posting, trust, negotiation, payment, reporting, saving, deleting, and message recall. The tone is short and direct so the answers can work in an accordion-style help page. It is designed to reduce uncertainty and help users understand the basic rules of the platform without reading long instructions.

### 4.4 Wanted Board Sample Content

The wanted board is written for users who are searching for something specific rather than browsing listings. It explains that students can post requests with a budget, category, pickup point, and short description so sellers know what they are looking for. The page focuses on clarity and response speed, which makes the request flow feel useful instead of repetitive.

### 4.5 Checkout Sample Content

The checkout page confirms the selected items and asks for contact and pick-up details. It is written as a simple transaction step that helps students finalize a campus handoff without leaving the platform. The confirmation message and order ID reinforce that the request has been submitted and is ready for seller review.

## 5. Design Rationale

The design focuses on trust, clarity, and student relevance.

Trust is supported by campus verification, seller ratings, pickup guidance, and a consistent handoff flow.

Clarity comes from a limited set of primary actions and a strong separation between browsing, selling, and requesting.

Student relevance is built through campus-specific categories, meeting points, textbook and dorm-use scenarios, and a wanted board for users who are searching rather than selling.

The interface also aims to feel visually polished. The transparency, gradients, and card depth create a showcase-style aesthetic, while the content hierarchy keeps the page useful rather than decorative.

## 6. Short Presentation Version

CampuX is a campus-only second-hand marketplace for students. It includes browsing, posting, wanted requests, checkout, messaging, and order tracking in one product. The homepage highlights the platform’s value proposition, the marketplace supports search and filtering, the Seller Studio helps users publish items quickly, and the Wanted Board lets students request items they need. The design emphasizes trust, speed, and a premium showcase-style visual language.
